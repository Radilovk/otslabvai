Methodology

How a BioCode peptide gets made.

From sequence design to a sealed, lot-tested vial: our process leans on standard, well-established peptide chemistry — executed with tight tolerances and documented at every checkpoint.

Process Overview

Four checkpoints, every lot

01

Design & Specification

Target sequence, expected molecular weight, and acceptance criteria (purity, identity, appearance) are documented before synthesis begins.

02

Fmoc Solid-Phase Synthesis

The peptide chain is assembled residue-by-residue on a solid resin support using Fmoc-protected amino acids — the standard approach across research and pharmaceutical peptide chemistry.

03

Cleavage & Preparative HPLC

Crude peptide is cleaved from resin, then purified by reverse-phase preparative HPLC to separate the target sequence from truncation/deletion side-products.

04

MS Identity & HPLC Purity Release Testing

Mass spectrometry confirms molecular identity; analytical HPLC quantifies purity. Results are compiled into a lot-specific Certificate of Analysis before release.

Why It Matters

Peptide science, briefly

Peptides are short chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds — smaller than proteins, but capable of highly specific biological interactions, which is exactly why they're such versatile tools in research: as receptor ligands, enzyme substrates or inhibitors, and structural probes.

That same specificity means small synthesis errors matter. A single truncated or deleted residue can change a peptide's behavior in an assay. This is why purification and identity confirmation aren't optional steps in our process — they're the steps that make a result trustworthy.

We publish typical purity specifications (generally ≥99% by HPLC) on every product page, and back each claim with lot-specific analytical data rather than a blanket catalog-wide assertion.

Reading a Certificate of Analysis

  • Lot number — ties the certificate to the specific batch shipped.
  • Identity (MS) — observed vs. theoretical molecular weight.
  • Purity (HPLC) — percentage of main peak area at a stated wavelength.
  • Appearance & solubility — physical description and recommended reconstitution solvent.
  • Storage conditions — recommended handling to preserve stability.

From Bench to Data

Every specification starts as bench chemistry

Behind each printed acceptance criterion is a physical measurement — a titration, an HPLC injection, a mass spec run. We treat the structural diagram and the wet-lab result as two views of the same fact, not marketing shorthand for one another.
